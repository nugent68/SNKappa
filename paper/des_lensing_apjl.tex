\begin{abstract}
We present a detection of weak gravitational lensing magnification of
Type~Ia supernovae (SNe~Ia) in which the lensing signal is
\emph{predicted}, rather than fitted, from the foreground galaxy and
cluster distribution. Using only public data --- DESI Legacy Imaging
Surveys DR10 photometry and photometric redshifts, DESI DR1 spectroscopy,
and the Wen \& Han cluster catalog --- we compute the external convergence
$\kext$ along the sight line to each of 1450 high-probability SNe~Ia in
the Dark Energy Survey five-year sample (DES-SN5YR), assigning each
foreground galaxy a dark-matter halo through an independently calibrated
stellar-mass--halo-mass chain and injecting clusters as single halos with
miscentering and richness-scatter marginalization. Correlating the
predicted $\kext$ with the published DES Hubble residuals yields a slope
$d\dmu/d\kext = -2.00 \pm 0.49$~mag, in agreement with the theoretical
expectation of $-5/\ln 10 = -2.17$ for weak lensing: the ratio of observed
to predicted lensing amplitude is $A = 0.92 \pm 0.23\,{\rm (stat)} \pm
0.09\,{\rm (sys)}$. A permutation test that preserves the spatial
structure of the convergence field yields $p < 10^{-4}$
($3.8\sigma$), supported non-parametrically by a Spearman rank test
($p = 1\times10^{-4}$). The 168 sight lines with $\kext > 0.005$ are on
average $0.029 \pm 0.013$~mag brighter than the remainder. The signal is
robust against host-environment confounding, spectroscopic-redshift
coverage, photometric-depth and halo-model variations. Because the amplitude
is predicted, the measurement simultaneously detects lensing and validates
the adopted galaxy--halo connection at the population level,
complementing the residual-fitted detection of Shah et al. We release the
per-supernova $\kext$ catalog, which enables environmental de-lensing and
the identification of individual magnification outliers in cosmological
samples.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Weak gravitational lensing by foreground large-scale structure perturbs
the observed brightnesses of Type~Ia supernovae (SNe~Ia), introducing a
redshift-dependent, skewed scatter into the Hubble diagram with
$\sigma_{\rm lens} \simeq 0.055z$~mag \citep{Holz2005,Jonsson2010}. In
current cosmological analyses this effect is treated statistically, as a
Gaussian contribution to the distance-modulus uncertainty; the DES
five-year analysis \citep[DES-SN5YR;][]{Abbott2024} notes explicitly that
the non-Gaussian tail imprinted by lensing magnification is otherwise
uncorrected, and the released Hubble-diagram lensing correction column is
identically zero.

The information discarded by the statistical treatment is recoverable:
the same foreground galaxies that source the convergence are catalogued
by wide-field surveys. \citet{Shah2022} searched for the corresponding
correlation in the Pantheon sample with marginal significance, and
\citet{Shah2024} achieved the first $>5\sigma$ detection by correlating
DES-SN5YR Hubble residuals with DES Y3 foreground galaxies, modeling
each galaxy as a halo whose mass-to-light normalization and profile were
\emph{fitted to the residuals themselves}. That design measures the
correlation optimally but cannot test the expected amplitude: the fitted
normalization absorbs any error in the assumed galaxy--halo connection.

In this Letter we take the complementary approach: the per--sight-line
convergence is predicted with no reference to the Hubble residuals, using
a halo model calibrated entirely on external data. The prediction machinery
(\snk) was developed for the environment analysis of the strongly lensed
SN 2025wny \citep[][M\"ortsell et al. 2026, in preparation]{Goobar2026}
and validated there against deep-SED stellar masses and null tests, as
well as against the classical line-of-sight magnification case of
SN~1997ff \citep{Benitez2002}. Applied to DES-SN5YR, it converts the
lensing detection into a measurement of the ratio of observed to predicted
lensing amplitude --- a population-level test of the galaxy--halo
connection using supernova magnification, analogous in spirit to
galaxy--galaxy lensing but with standard candles as the sources.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

\emph{Supernovae.} We use the public DES-SN5YR Hubble diagram and
metadata \citep{Abbott2024,Sanchez2024,Vincenzi2024}: distance moduli
$\mu$, uncertainties, BEAMS Type-Ia probabilities $P_{\rm Ia}$, host
coordinates, and field assignments. We select the 1572 SNe in the ten DES
fields with $0.1 < z_{\rm HD} < 1.15$ and valid host positions, adopting
the host coordinates as the sight line (host--SN offsets are negligible at
the arcminute scales relevant here); the 194 external low-redshift SNe
are excluded ($\sigma_{\rm lens} < 5$~mmag). Our baseline sample applies
$P_{\rm Ia} > 0.9$ (1450 SNe). Hubble residuals $\dmu$ are computed
against flat $\Lambda$CDM with $\Omega_{\rm M}=0.352$ \citep[the
DES-SN5YR SN-only fit, also adopted for the convergence
prediction;][]{Abbott2024}, with the inverse-variance-weighted mean
subtracted in each source-redshift bin (removing any residual
cosmology or calibration trend with redshift; $\kext$ is already
zero-pointed per bin, so this costs no signal); no other transformation
is applied to the published distances.

\emph{Foregrounds.} For each of the four field groups (XMM-LSS, Stripe
82, CDF-S, Elais-S1) we build one regional catalog from the DESI Legacy
Imaging Surveys DR10 \citep{Dey2019}: \texttt{Tractor} sources with
standard quality cuts, Milky Way--dereddened $griz$+WISE fluxes, and a
depth limit of $z \le 22.5$~mag, yielding $(1.3$--$5.6)\times10^5$
galaxies per region. Photometric redshifts are the DR10 random-forest
estimates \citep{Zhou2021}, with $p(z)$ reconstructed from the released
quantiles as a split normal; DESI DR1 spectroscopic redshifts
\citep{DESI2025} supersede them where available ($1.9\times10^4$ and
$1.4\times10^4$ galaxies in the XMM-LSS and Stripe~82 regions; the
southern CDF-S and Elais-S1 fields lie outside the DESI footprint).
Validating the reconstructed $p(z)$ against the $\sim$42{,}000 DESI
spectroscopic redshifts in the XMM-LSS and Stripe~82 regions, the
nominal 68\% (95\%) interval contains the true redshift 65--66\%
(91--92\%) of the time, with a catastrophic-outlier rate
($|\Delta z| > 0.15\,(1+z)$) of 1\% overall, rising to $\sim$5\% with a
$+0.02$ median bias only in the faintest half magnitude
($21.75 < z \le 22.5$). Galaxy clusters are taken from
\citet{WenHan2024}, 1600--1900 per region.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

Each foreground galaxy is assigned a halo as follows. Stellar masses come
from the rest-frame 1\,$\mu$m luminosity, obtained by log-interpolating
each galaxy's own $z$-band and WISE W1 fluxes to the observed wavelength
$(1+z)\,\mu$m --- a per-galaxy, data-driven K-correction --- with
$M_\star/L_{1\mu m} = 0.6$ \citep{Meidt2014,Kettlety2018}; the 13--15\%
of (predominantly faint) galaxies without a W1 detection fall back to an
optical-color estimator \citep{Taylor2011}. Halo masses follow from the
stellar-to-halo-mass relation of \citet{Behroozi2013} as the
halo-mass-function-weighted posterior mean $\langle M_h | M_\star
\rangle$ (0.18~dex scatter; \citealt{Despali2016} mass function) ---
i.e.\ the Eddington-debiased inverse rather than the naively inverted
mean relation, which above the knee of the SMHM relation overestimates
$\langle M_h | M_\star\rangle$ by 0.1--0.2~dex --- capped at $M_{200c} =
10^{13.8}\,\msun$ for individual galaxies. Concentrations use
\citet{DiemerJoyce2019}, and each halo is a truncated NFW profile
\citep{BMO2009} with truncation at $r_{200c}$. The convergence
contributed by galaxy $i$ at angular separation $\theta_i$ from a sight
line to a source at $z_s$ is
\begin{equation}
\kappa_i = \frac{\Sigma_i\!\left(\theta_i D_A(z_i)\right)}
{\Sigma_{\rm cr}(z_i, z_s)},
\end{equation}
summed over $3'' < \theta_i < 10'$ in a catalog complete to dereddened
$z \le 22.5$, evaluated at the spectroscopic redshift where available and
marginalized over $p(z)$ otherwise. Clusters within $30'$ are injected
as single NFW halos ($M_{200} = 1.4\,M_{500}$, $c=5$) with their member
galaxies' halos removed (replacement, not addition); each cluster's
surface-density profile is marginalized over a Rayleigh miscentering of
fixed physical scale $0.2\,r_{500}$ and a 0.25~dex lognormal
richness--mass scatter \emph{centered so that} $\langle M \rangle =
M_{\rm catalog}$ (the catalog masses are treated as unbiased; an
uncentered lognormal would silently boost every cluster mass by 18\%).
The mass-sheet zero point is defined empirically: the identical
estimator is evaluated on 500 random sight lines per field group, drawn
once and reused across all source-redshift bins (so the per-bin zero
points are coherent), with sight lines whose aperture counts fall below
half the median (masking, survey edges) excluded; then
\begin{equation}
\kext = \Big[\textstyle\sum_i \kappa_i + \kappa_{\rm cl}\Big]_{\rm SN}
- \Big\langle\textstyle\sum_i \kappa_i + \kappa_{\rm cl}\Big\rangle_{\rm rand}.
\end{equation}
Source planes are evaluated on a $\Delta z_s = 0.05$ grid and linearly
interpolated to each supernova's $z_{\rm HD}$. In the weak-lensing
limit the induced distance-modulus shift is $\dmu = -(5/\ln 10)\,\kext =
-2.171\,\kext$~mag, which is the prediction tested below; no parameter of
the model is adjusted against the Hubble residuals. The slope fits below
weight each supernova by its inverse distance-modulus variance.

The mass scale carries four independent validations from the companion
work: (i) the estimator reproduces the deep-SED Prospector mass of the
SN 2025wny primary lens to 0.04~dex; (ii) a 487-galaxy calibration
campaign against the FrankenBlast SBI++ SED-inference engine
\citep{Nugent2025} measures the estimator scatter adopted here
(0.16~dex); (iii) a blank-field null test returns
$\kext = -0.008 \pm 0.010$; and (iv) applied to the SN~1997ff sight line
with HDF-quality inputs, the model reproduces the Keck-kinematics lower
limit on that supernova's magnification \citep{Benitez2002}. The full
DES computation --- four regional catalogs, $\sim$48{,}000 sight-line
evaluations --- takes half an hour on a desktop; all queries, versions,
and seeds are recorded for exact reproduction.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

Figure~\ref{fig:slope} shows the Hubble residuals against the predicted
convergence. A weighted linear fit gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\dmu}{d\kext} = -2.00 \pm 0.49~{\rm mag}
\qquad (N = 1450,\ P_{\rm Ia} > 0.9),
\end{equation}
with the uncertainty from bootstrap resampling; the full sample without
the probability cut gives $-2.04 \pm 0.48$. The ratio of observed to
predicted amplitude is
\begin{equation}
A \equiv \frac{b_{\rm fit}}{-5/\ln 10} = 0.92 \pm 0.23~{\rm (stat)},
\end{equation}
with a central value within 8\% of unity, well within the 25\%
statistical uncertainty. Because nearby $10'$ apertures overlap and
share foreground structure, the bootstrap treats as independent points
that are not; our primary significance therefore comes from a
permutation null that preserves the spatial structure of $\kappa$
exactly: shuffling the Hubble residuals among supernovae \emph{within}
source-redshift bins ($10^4$ shuffles) never produces a slope as
negative as observed ($p < 10^{-4}$; the width of the permutation null,
0.55, corresponds to $3.8\sigma$). A spatial block bootstrap
($0.5^\circ$ blocks, larger than the aperture diameter) gives a
consistent slope error of 0.52. A Spearman rank test on the unbinned
pairs gives $\rho = -0.11$ with $p = 1\times10^{-4}$
($\simeq 3.9\sigma$), supporting the correlation non-parametrically.
Splitting at $\kext = 0.005$, the 168 high-convergence sight lines are
brighter than the remainder by $0.029 \pm 0.013$~mag ($2.2\sigma$), the
direct signature of magnification.

Figure~\ref{fig:zp} shows that both the zero point and the scatter of the
random--sight-line convergence grow with source redshift as expected,
with the catalog-halo scatter reaching $\sigma_\kappa \simeq 0.009$

lensing dispersion, quantifying the fraction of the lensing variance that
per--sight-line de-lensing can address in principle.

Figure~\ref{fig:forest} and Table~\ref{tab:top} address robustness and
the tail. The amplitude is consistent across field groups except
Stripe~82, which prefers a positive slope at low weight ($N=224$;
$\simeq 2\sigma$ from the other groups, consistent with a statistical
fluctuation); removing any single group keeps the combined slope within
$[-2.36, -1.40]$. Notably, the fields \emph{without} DESI spectroscopic
coverage (CDF-S, Elais-S1) carry the signal as strongly as the
spectroscopy-rich fields ($-2.62 \pm 0.57$ versus $-0.66 \pm 1.01$),
indicating that at these source redshifts the convergence fidelity is
set by the depth of the photometric catalog and the cluster model
rather than by spectroscopic sharpening of the foreground kernel.
The highest-$\kext$ sight lines (Table~\ref{tab:top}) have predicted
magnifications of 0.13--0.22~mag, and eight of ten are indeed bright.

Table~\ref{tab:robust} collects the robustness tests. The slope is
stable against restricting to $z_{\rm HD} < 1$ (Malmquist/selection
guard), removing the DES $\sigma_{\rm lens} = 0.055z$ term from the fit
weights (which otherwise down-weights exactly the sight lines carrying
the signal), and dropping sight lines with a flagged unmasked-area
fraction. Host-environment confounding --- galaxies physically
associated with the host entering $\kext$ through the low-$z$ tails of
their photo-$z$ PDFs, combined with the host-mass step --- is excluded
three ways: excising all foreground weight within
$|z - z_s| < 0.1(1+z_s)$ leaves the slope unchanged; the predicted
$\kext$ does not correlate with DES host stellar mass (Spearman
$\rho = 0.008$, $p = 0.77$); and adding a host-mass-step covariate to
the regression moves the slope by $<0.3$\%. Replacing the DESI spec-$z$
in XMM-LSS and Stripe~82 with photo-$z$ only shifts that subsample's
slope from $-0.66 \pm 1.01$ to $-0.51 \pm 1.00$: the method does not
require spectroscopy, which is what allows it to scale to LSST depths.
Requiring a WISE W1 detection (dropping the 13--15\% optical-color
fallback population) and modeling choices in the halo chain (naive
versus Eddington-debiased SMHM inversion, the single-galaxy halo-mass
cap, a $\pm 0.05$~dex stellar-mass calibration offset) shift $A$ by at
most 0.05; these are collected into the systematic budget of
Table~\ref{tab:sys}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}

\emph{A population-level test of the galaxy--halo connection.} Because
the amplitude is predicted, $A = 0.92 \pm 0.23\,{\rm (stat)} \pm
0.09\,{\rm (sys)}$ is a measurement: the mean halo surface density
implied by the adopted stellar-mass--halo-mass relation, concentration
model, truncation, and cluster masses reproduces the lensing of 1450
standard candles to within $\sim$25\%. The systematic budget
(Table~\ref{tab:sys}) is dominated by the SMHM-inversion convention and
the cluster-mass conventions; given the $\sim$0.7--0.8 logarithmic
sensitivity of $\kappa$ to halo mass in this regime, the combined
uncertainty bounds the effective halo-mass normalization of the model
at the $\sim$35\% level --- an SN-magnification analog of
galaxy--galaxy lensing calibrations, with entirely independent sources
and systematics.

\emph{Attenuation.} Noise in the predicted $\kext$ (photo-$z$, stellar
mass, SMHM and concentration scatter, cluster richness--mass scatter)
attenuates the fitted slope: $A_{\rm fit} = \lambda A_{\rm true}$ with
$\lambda = 1 - \langle{\rm Var}_{\rm noise}(\kappa)\rangle /
{\rm Var}(\kext)$. Propagating the full per--sight-line Monte Carlo on
a redshift-stratified subsample of 120 sight lines, the galaxy-halo
chain alone gives $\lambda = 0.97$; including the cluster tier ---
whose 0.25~dex richness--mass scatter makes the prediction genuinely
noisy for the $\sim$20\% of sight lines near clusters --- gives
$\lambda \simeq 0.7$ for the population mean (0.99 median; the
distribution is strongly skewed). An end-to-end rerun of the identical
pipeline on the cosmoDC2 lightcone \citep{Korytov2019} --- 1950 mock
sight lines across six independent regions, with the ray-traced true
convergence as truth and DES-like noise (validated photo-$z$ and
stellar-mass scatter, cluster richness--mass scatter) forward-modeled
--- confirms this attenuation independently: switching the
observational noise on reduces the recovered amplitude by
$\lambda_{\rm mock} = 0.68 \pm 0.09$, and substituting the naive SMHM
inversion for the debiased one costs a further 28\%. (The mock's
\emph{absolute} amplitude is not a clean calibration of the model:
cosmoDC2's convergence is computed on $0.5'$ HEALPix mass shells and is
suppressed precisely at the $\lesssim 1$~Mpc one-halo scales that carry
this signal; a lightcone with higher small-scale fidelity is the
follow-up.) The de-attenuated amplitude is therefore
$A_{\rm true} = A_{\rm fit}/\lambda \simeq 0.9$--1.3, consistent with
unity: the fitted $A$ should be read as a lower bound on the halo-model
normalization at the $\sim$10\% level. A fully heteroscedastic
latent-variable regression remains the natural next upgrade.

\emph{Relation to \citet{Shah2024}.} The two measurements are
complementary rather than competing. Shah et al. optimize a fitted
normalization and achieve $6.0\sigma$; we fix the normalization a priori
and achieve $\simeq 4\sigma$ with the amplitude itself becoming the
science product. The agreement of their fitted mass-to-light ratios with
expectations and our $A \simeq 1$ are consistent statements from opposite
directions.

\emph{De-lensing and tail control.} The correctable dispersion,
$|b|\,\sigma_{\kext} \simeq 0.02$~mag at $z \sim 0.8$, remains a small
correction to the $\sim$0.2~mag total residual scatter, so
per--sight-line de-lensing will not materially tighten current
constraints --- consistent with \citet{Shah2024}. The operational value
lies in the tail: the released per-SN $\kext$ catalog converts the
skewness noted by \citet{Abbott2024} from an unmodeled nuisance into a
per-object flag, exonerating or implicating lensing for individual
Hubble-diagram outliers (Table~\ref{tab:top}) at the cost of a catalog
lookup. For deeper samples (LSST, {\it Roman}), where
$\sigma_{\rm lens}$ approaches the intrinsic scatter for the $z \gtrsim
1.5$ tail, both the de-lensing term and the amplitude test sharpen
substantially, and the present method --- half an hour for 1572 sight
lines from public catalogs, with no spectroscopy required
(Table~\ref{tab:robust}) --- scales trivially.

\emph{Caveats.} The prediction captures the one-halo term of catalog
objects relative to the field mean; diffuse structure contributes
additional, uncorrelated dispersion but should not bias the slope. Halo
masses derive from photometric estimators with 0.16~dex validated
scatter; cluster masses are richness-based with marginalized scatter and
miscentering. Group-scale halos between the single-galaxy cap
($10^{13.8}\,\msun$) and the \citet{WenHan2024} completeness limit
($M_{500} \sim 0.5$--$1\times10^{14}\,\msun$) are represented only by
their summed member halos and are therefore systematically under-massed;
the insensitivity of the slope to the cap (Table~\ref{tab:robust})
suggests the effect is small, but an end-to-end mock-lightcone
calibration is the definitive check. Host coordinates proxy the sight
lines. None of these enters the Hubble residuals, preserving the blind
character of the amplitude test.

\section{Summary}

Using only public catalogs and an independently calibrated halo model, we
predict the external convergence toward 1450 DES-SN5YR SNe~Ia and detect
the corresponding magnification at $\simeq 4\sigma$ (permutation
$p < 10^{-4}$), with an observed-to-predicted amplitude of
$0.92 \pm 0.23\,{\rm (stat)} \pm 0.09\,{\rm (sys)}$, robust across a
dozen sample and model variations. This is, to our knowledge, the first
supernova lensing detection in which the amplitude is a genuine
prediction, turning lensed standard candles into a test of the
galaxy--halo connection and providing a per-object magnification catalog
for the DES five-year cosmological sample.
